# Commit To Blue: World-Building and Drift in White Southern Poetry

## Abstract

*Commit To Blue* studies a particular white Southern poetic and critical lineage rather than Southern letters in the aggregate. Its argument is that this lineage is best understood as a repeated attempt to construct a coherent formal world under conditions of mismatch, rupture, memorial repair, overload, and eventual drift. The book begins with antebellum failures of formal localization, turns to Poe as the first decisive constructor of a closed Southern atmospheric world, follows the memorial and stabilizing work of Confederate elegy and Fugitive-Agrarian reconstruction, and then tracks the later afterlives of that line in littoral counterformations, Frank Stanford, James Dickey, C. D. Wright, Dave Smith, Ellen Bryant Voigt, and Miller Williams.

The project is deliberately hybrid. It is a critical monograph with a built-in anthology spine. Each chapter is paired with a compact field of poems, essays, periodical materials, memorial documents, or little-magazine evidence that demonstrates the transformation under discussion. The anthology is not supplementary. It is the second structure by which the argument is made legible.

## Scope

This is not a history of Southern poetry as a whole. It does not claim to map the full Southern field, nor does it pretend that a white poetic line is identical with Southern literature. Its object is narrower: a white Southern poetic and critical inheritance running from antebellum efforts at formal localization through Poe, Confederate memorial verse, white Southern modernist divergence, Fugitive and Agrarian reconstruction, the white Gulf and New Orleans counterline, and selected late-twentieth-century afterlives.

That narrowing changes the language of the project. Terms such as `Southern letters`, `Southern writing`, and `Southern poetry` can only be used with precision and restraint. Where the book speaks broadly, it does so as shorthand for one white Southern line, not for the field entire.

## Core Argument

The book rests on one governing claim: the most coherent white Southern poetic line is best read as a sequence of attempts to build and preserve formal worldhood under conditions hostile to durable coherence. The early white Southern field struggles to localize inherited forms. Poe solves that problem by closure. Confederate elegy attempts to stabilize a shattered white social world through memorial form. Fletcher marks a white modernist divergence that never becomes the dominant line. The Fugitives and Agrarians rebuild white Southern coherence through criticism, poetry, and institutional consolidation. The later field retains force but loses totality; it survives as drift, counterline, scene, and afterlife rather than sovereign tradition.

The book therefore moves between close reading, intellectual history, periodical culture, memorial infrastructure, and archival recovery. It uses `world` in the strict sense of a structured field of significance rather than as a synonym for setting or atmosphere alone.

## Method

The project combines formalist literary criticism, archive-conscious regional historiography, and anthology-based argument. Its evidence is ordered in four layers:

1. Primary literary texts
2. Contemporaneous literary-public or institutional materials
3. Later criticism and historiography
4. Archival or special-collections targets where the printed record is thin or unstable

Three distinctions govern the method:

1. Structural analogy is not the same as documented influence.
2. Institutional continuity is not the same as uninterrupted literary coherence.
3. Abundance in the archive is not the same as innocence, and absence is not the same as nonexistence.

The project keeps the archive honest by naming what it excludes. It does not erase Black Southern poetics, blues traditions, or broader Gulf South countertraditions; it simply does not make them the evidentiary center of this study.

## Why This Book

The field has strong single-author studies, strong books on the Southern Renaissance, and strong revisionist works on race, memory, and regional identity. What it lacks is a synthetic account of one white Southern poetic and critical line from antebellum mismatch to late postsouthern afterlife, written with enough formal pressure to connect poetry, criticism, memorial culture, little magazines, and institutional succession without flattening them into survey history.

This project also answers a practical need. Because its chapters are built around compact source clusters, it can function both as a scholarly monograph and as a teachable course text. The anthology spine gives it use value in classrooms without turning it into a neutral survey anthology.

## Distinctive Features

- It treats Poe not merely as Gothic precursor but as the first decisive builder of a closed Southern formal world.
- It reads white Confederate elegy as a memorial technology rather than as free-floating pathos.
- It places Fletcher at the center of a white Southern modernist divergence that did not become the dominant line.
- It treats Fugitive and Agrarian formalism as world-repair rather than as mere school formation.
- It reconstructs a white Gulf and New Orleans counterline rather than collapsing all white Southern coherence into inland pastoral or Vanderbilt formalism.
- It carries the line forward through Stanford, Dickey, C. D. Wright, Dave Smith, Voigt, and Miller Williams without pretending that this exhausts the late Southern field.

## Chapter Spine

### Introduction: Blue, Worldhood, and White Southern Form

The introduction defines `blue` without relying on a full blues-theoretical frame the book is not actually following. Here blue names cooling, elegy, atmosphere, tonal residue, and unresolved formal pressure within the white Southern line. The conceptual apparatus comes from ontology, historical time, white sacred print culture, and white critical self-consciousness rather than from a broad Black Southern acoustic field.

### Prologue: *Wuthering Heights* and the House-Landscape Machine

The prologue is a structural prefiguration, not a claim of direct lineage. Bronte clarifies a formal problem central to later white Southern writing: how house, bloodline, weather, enclosure, and topography fuse into a single world-bearing machine.

### Chapter 1: The Pre-Gothic Primordial

This chapter reconstructs a white antebellum poetic field that struggles to localize inherited forms. It expands beyond a few familiar poems to include magazines, reviews, minor poets, devotional print, and sacred tunebooks as evidence of a white Southern expressive infrastructure still trying to discover its own formal ground.

### Chapter 2: Poe and the Closed Formal World

Poe is the first decisive solution to the problem of incoherence. He builds a sealed atmosphere in which enclosure, effect, dynastic ruin, and designed interiority achieve a level of formal coherence the earlier white Southern field had not yet reached.

### Chapter 3: Confederate Rupture and White Memorial Stabilization

This chapter studies white Confederate memorial poetry and its institutions as a stabilization system built after defeat. Timrod, Ryan, Hayne, cemetery ritual, memorial associations, inscriptions, and commemorative print culture together form a white memorial-poetic apparatus rather than a handful of isolated texts.

### Chapter 4: Fletcher and the White Southern Modernist Divergence

Fletcher marks a real divergence from memorial closure and later Agrarian consolidation. This chapter narrows accordingly: it is not a survey of multiple Southern modernisms, but a white Southern modernist possibility centered on Fletcher, Imagism, little-magazine culture, and adjacent white print networks.

### Chapter 5: Ransom, Tate, the Fugitives, and Agrarian World-Repair

This is the project's central repair chapter. The Fugitives and Agrarians rebuild white Southern coherence through poetry, criticism, pedagogy, and group self-institutionalization. The chapter treats their abundance in the archive as part of the story of canon power.

### Chapter 6: The White Gulf and New Orleans Counterline

This chapter preserves the littoral alternative without overclaiming New Orleans in full. Its subject is a white Gulf and New Orleans counterline running through Cable, Chopin, Hearn if needed, *The Double Dealer*, Everette Maddox, the Maple Leaf line, and selected later white New Orleans poets.

### Chapter 7: Frank Stanford and James Dickey as Pressure Tests

Stanford and Dickey remain the strongest late pressure cases in the project. Stanford overloads the line from within; Dickey forces it open through body, risk, velocity, and exposure. Together they show distortion, pressure, and afterlife rather than simple collapse.

### Chapter 8: Terminal Drift and the White Postsouthern Afterlife

The final chapter follows a late white poetic and institutional afterlife through C. D. Wright, Dave Smith, Ellen Bryant Voigt, Miller Williams, and the institutions and magazines that helped sustain this line after its period of strongest sovereignty.

## Anthology and Permissions Plan

The anthology spine remains viable under the narrowed scope. The core packets are:

- Bronte and Poe
- White antebellum poetry, criticism, and sacred print
- Confederate memorial verse and association materials
- Fletcher and white modernist divergence
- Fugitive and Agrarian consolidation
- White Gulf and New Orleans counterline
- Stanford and Dickey
- White postsouthern afterlife

The preferred format remains a critical book with compact anthology materials integrated by chapter or gathered in a concluding apparatus. Public-domain materials anchor the early chapters; later twentieth-century poetry requires selective permissions planning.
