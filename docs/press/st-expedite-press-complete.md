# ST EXPEDITE PRESS

## Complete Document of Mission, Program, Operations, and Horizon

St. Expedite Press is the publishing arm of the larger Sandbatch project. It is a New Orleans press organized around one hard claim: serious literary work now requires institutions willing to act outside the approval systems that flatten it. The press publishes books, builds editorial pressure, recovers neglected lines of continuity, tests conceptual instruments, and prepares the conditions for a literary ecology large enough to outlast its founder without being captured by the machinery it was built to evade.

This document is the canonical synthesis of the press as it presently exists and as it intends to exist. It consolidates mission language, website posture, catalog architecture, operational doctrine, editorial standards, research priorities, economic logic, and long-horizon institutional design. The older press support folders have already been collapsed into this file; the deeper theory that feeds the press now lives under `Thinking/`, where it belongs.

## I. Present Fact Pattern

St. Expedite Press is no longer an abstract proposal. It already has a live public surface. The site at `stexpedite.press` presently exposes a portal, a books page, a mission/about page, a lab page, a services page, a donation page, a submission page, a general contact page, and a storefront route. The visual and textual key is already clear: New Orleans coordinates, night-facing atmosphere, direct address, first-party email capture, and a refusal to pretend that a small press needs to look like a mid-sized corporate publisher in order to be real.

The present site already establishes several governing truths. The catalog is framed as the program rather than as an inventory dump. The mission language is legible and forceful. The donation page is stripped down to its premise: the money goes to the work. The services page positions advisory engagements as selective extensions of editorial discipline rather than as a consulting business seeking literary camouflage. The lab is active, with the American Anglossic Compass functioning as a public instrument rather than a placeholder. The submission and contact surfaces are cleanly separated, which matters because institutions decay first in routing.

In other words, phase one is not hypothetical. A presence already exists. The immediate task is to deepen it until it becomes unmistakably an institution.

## II. Origin and Disposition

The patron is Saint Expedite, invoked in urgent causes when delay has itself become part of the injury. That is not decorative patronage. It states the operating tempo. The press is not organized around patient credential accumulation, patient grant cultivation, or patient waiting for permission from people whose own judgment has already been deformed by institutional drift. It is organized around intervention, directness, and the willingness to move while the official world is still assembling its forms.

The governing myth remains Osiris gathered back together by Isis. Southern writing, in this frame, is not absent because it failed to produce greatness. It has been scattered by trade publishing, by academic sanitation, by regional caricature, by the collapse of local houses, by class embarrassment, and by the general American habit of preserving only those fragments that can be repackaged without danger. The task of the press is therefore reconstructive. It gathers what was severed, names what was misfiled, restores circulation where circulation stopped, and prints new work capable of joining the old line without servility toward it.

This makes the press anti-nostalgic by definition. Nostalgia turns the South into décor, heritage product, or managed regret. St. Expedite Press is interested in living pressure, not in costume. It is equally non-neutral about standards. The work either earns itself or it does not. There is no substitute metric. And it is oriented toward continuity rather than one-off release behavior. A good title matters, but a coherent sequence of titles matters more because it forms a legible civilization-scale argument about what should remain available.

## III. Editorial Doctrine

The public language of the press can be summarized as soft Catholic, postpolitical, postidentitarian, Gulf Gothic, folk noir, night-minded, and hostile to flattening. Each of those terms names a real discipline. None of them is branding vapor.

Soft Catholic does not mean confessional gatekeeping. It means the press is interested in sacramental residue, moral consequence, grotesque revelation, eschatological pressure, and the stubborn fact that acts exceed the psychologized explanations modern prose likes to offer for them. The manuscript need not be pious. It does need to know that the world is thicker than sociology.

Postpolitical means that the press refuses to let contemporary sorting mechanisms do the editorial reading for it. This is not a denial that politics exists. It is a refusal to reduce the work to a political résumé. Books are selected by sentence pressure, tonal command, scene architecture, intellectual seriousness, and the degree to which they can bear rereading without collapsing into topical utility. If a work irritates both official progressives and official conservatives, that is often evidence that it has escaped the sorting machine.

Postidentitarian means that the press will take place, lineage, class formation, language, region, and spiritual inheritance seriously while refusing demographic credential logic as an editorial proxy. The South is not a diversity slot. It is a dense historical and metaphysical zone. So are New Orleans, the Gulf Coast, the Delta, and the longleaf world. The press reads manuscripts, archives, and sentences. It does not read grant-speak identity summaries and mistake that for discernment.

The public aesthetic remains Gulf Gothic or folk noir because those names correctly signal the atmosphere without exhausting it. Port city. Black water. Catholic afterglow. Market ruin. River commerce. Carnival residue. Heat-sick comedy. Civilizational decay without decorative despair. The site phrase "in the key of Night" is therefore not merely a tagline. It names the tonal register in which the press prefers to operate: nocturnal, lucid, unfooled, and willing to let beauty arrive by way of danger rather than uplift.

The terminal doctrine is simple. The work either earns its existence or it does not. No advisory board, no trend report, no regional piety, no solidarity rhetoric, and no social proof can rescue a weak book.

## IV. Catalog Architecture

The current public structure points to a press with three principal book programs, one periodical horizon, one experimental surface, one revenue bridge, and one much larger institutional horizon. That architecture is correct and should remain legible.

### SEXP

SEXP is the flagship original-work line. It is where the press publishes fiction, novella, poetry, and hybrid work that does not need the trade apparatus in order to make its case. The selection premise is not that the work be "Southern" in a chamber-of-commerce sense. The premise is that it carry formal and spiritual pressure strong enough to survive direct encounter. It should know the tradition without being trapped in homage, and it should prefer density to bloat. The present title cluster already implies the shape: `Lift Wind / Love Heat: Symphony No. 1 in C Minor`, `The Fevers and the Humors`, and the John Slaughter pipeline indicate a list interested in fever, weather, moral extremity, and musical structure rather than lifestyle realism or workshop midwittery.

SEXP should remain a short list by design. A press this small cannot afford books that merely occupy space. Each entry must alter the perceived shape of the whole catalog. The ideal SEXP title can live two lives at once: first as a direct-sale object that a reader actively seeks, and later as a long-tail title that continues to radiate seriousness after the launch window has died.

### Library of the Southern Civilization

The Library is the recovery arm. It is not antiquarianism and it is not heritage display. It is a program for repairing memory where memory has been deformed by Northern editorial taste, academic rehabilitation programs that defang what they canonize, and commercial catalog logic that cannot tell the difference between a living minor classic and a dead curio. When the books page says the catalog is the program, this line is one reason why. Recovery is not supplemental to the mission. It is one of the mission's main proofs.

The named priority cluster remains sound: George S. Patton Jr.'s poems, Donald Davidson's `The Tall Men`, the early books of John Gould Fletcher, Genevieve Taggard volumes, and a John Peale Bishop title when the public-domain window properly opens. That list should be read as a method specimen rather than as a closed menu. The underlying test is whether the book sharpens the reader's sense of what Southern writing has been, what it has been denied permission to remain, and what future work might rejoin if the chain is repaired.

The research discipline here matters. The archive is not neutral. Survivals are political. Cataloging is political. OCR quality is political. A republication line worth respecting therefore needs explicit edition logic, provenance control, and textual honesty. If a source text survives only in damaged scans, that damage must be acknowledged. If variant editions exist, selection must be argued rather than smuggled in. If a book matters now only as a historical curiosity, it does not belong in the line. The Library is for live ammunition.

### European Classics and Modernists in Translation

The translation line exists because Spanish, French, and Russian letters preserved registers that Anglo-American publishing has spent decades flattening: theological grotesque, metaphysical ferocity, symbolic density, aristocratic or liturgical cadence, and the right to remain difficult without apologizing for it. The press should not translate for cultural tourism or syllabus service. It should translate because certain works preserve tools the Anglophone world has carelessly misplaced.

Cadence matters more than classroom smoothness. Pressure matters more than paraphrase. The translator is not in the business of laundering strangeness into respectable contemporary prose. A St. Expedite translation should read as though something dangerous has crossed the water intact. That does not authorize sloppiness. It means fidelity is measured at the level of force, syntax, temperature, and spiritual posture, not at the level of bloodless lexical substitution.

### RICE Magazine

The periodical line is now RICE Magazine. The rename matters and should be treated as complete. RICE is the quicker, more volatile, more recurrent public object through which the press can publish fiction, criticism, polemic, archival fragments, translations, visual matter, notebook pages, and other shorter work that does not need to wait for a bound book in order to earn a public life.

RICE should function as more than a container for miscellany. It is talent discovery, editorial assay, pressure valve, and institutional metronome. A serious press becomes more real when it can produce not only isolated books but rhythm. The catalog gives the press weight; the magazine gives it pulse. The name also fits the regional intelligence of the project. Rice is staple rather than luxury, field rather than ornament, Gulf-Coast and delta-adjacent rather than metropolitan. The magazine should feel like that: sustaining, deceptively plain, carrying heat.

The journal should be printed as an object the moment the economics justify it. No think-piece sludge, no generic "literary magazine" self-description, no endless online issue architecture meant to simulate seriousness while evading the cost of objecthood. If the catalog funds the journal, the journal can then expand the catalog's social field by bringing in contributors, correspondents, readers, and allied houses without surrendering the press's standards to institutional networking rituals.

### The Lab

The lab exists because not every serious object is a book. The live site already proves the case with the American Anglossic Compass, which is a field instrument rather than a manuscript. That distinction is important. The press needs a surface on which conceptual machines, tests, maps, interpretive protocols, and public-facing experiments can exist without being distorted into faux-books or stranded as private notes.

The lab also performs a second function: it is the visible membrane between publishing and theory. Work that begins in `Thinking/` may emerge through the lab before it stabilizes into a book, essay, or platform. The lab therefore prevents two bad outcomes at once. It prevents the press from pretending that every idea is already a title, and it prevents the theory layer from becoming an unpublished swamp.

### Services

The services page on the live site is correct in both concept and scale. Advisory work is not ornamental copy. It is a real revenue bridge and should remain one, but the press must keep it subordinated to the literary project. The number of external engagements should remain deliberately small. The principle is not growth. It is subsidy without capture.

The existing service lanes also make sense. Editorial architecture and publishing strategy translate the press's own institutional discipline into work for other organizations. Ontology and knowledge design apply the same rigor of definition and relation used in literary criticism to systems where traceability matters. Auditable decision-intelligence work extends that rigor into high-consequence workflows where provenance, reviewability, and governance matter more than novelty theater. There is no contradiction here. The same mind that can build a coherent catalog can help a serious institution think straight under pressure.

### Commonsplace

Commonsplace is not another series. It is the horizon at which the press stops behaving like a lone imprint and begins behaving like a founding node for a larger literary ecology. Its full doctrine lives in `COMMONSPLACES — Complete Document.md`, but its place here should be plainly stated. The press publishes books, RICE Magazine builds periodic pressure, the fellowship stabilizes time for writers, the lab prototypes instruments, and Commonsplace gathers those threads into canon infrastructure, fellowship architecture, venue logic, guild coordination, and eventually platform design.

## V. Institutional Form

The legal shell remains `St. Expedite Fellowship, L.L.C.`, Louisiana-based and sole-managed. That structure is not boilerplate. It expresses the doctrine in legal form: narrow enough to act, direct enough to preserve standards, and flexible enough to hold several publishing models without pretending that every title or collaboration must fit the same acquisition script. The refusal of an advisory board is not adolescent anti-institutional posture. It is recognition that most advisory structures in this space function as dilution mechanisms.

This does not mean the press is casual. Quite the opposite. Rights, licensing, contractual scope, venue discipline, and editorial responsibility should all be explicit. The press needs to be lean, not vague. It should be able to publish founder-led work, author-led work under negotiated terms, recovery editions requiring rights or text-establishment labor, translated works requiring layered permissions, and occasional service-heavy projects that make sense within the larger doctrine. Flexibility is not drift if the center holds.

## VI. Economic Logic

The press should continue to operate on mixed revenue. Direct title sales, objects and prints, donations, selective services, future subscriptions, and eventual periodical or fellowship support are not side ideas. They are the practical shape of independence for an institution too serious to entrust its continuity to grants, committees, prestige sponsorship, or speculative scale fantasies.

The economic thesis is straightforward. The catalog should fund itself first. Once the catalog has weight, it should help fund RICE Magazine. Once the catalog and journal together generate recurring surplus, that surplus should begin purchasing time for writers through a fellowship mechanism or equivalent supported-work model. Only after those layers are real should the press attempt any broader ecology play that assumes durable external coordination.

The donation surface already states the matter cleanly: the money goes to the work. That sentence should govern the whole economic culture of the institution. No donor theater. No ceremonial development language. Contributions are not gifts to an abstraction called literature. They are material support for books, objects, pages, time, printing, and continuity.

## VII. Public Surface and Messaging

The live site has already found its tone. The job now is to hold it consistently across every surface. The portal establishes the atmosphere. The books page argues that the catalog is the program. The about page carries the Osiris myth, the Saint Expedite patronage, the three-arm book program, and the sequence from presence to journal to fellowship. The lab page demonstrates that experimental instruments are already part of the institution rather than future vapor. The services page avoids corporate sludge and correctly describes the work as selective. The submit and contact pages have different jobs and should remain different. The store should remain direct and sparse enough that every object feels intentional.

Several principles follow. First, the about/mission page should remain the strongest prose on the site because it explains why the rest exists. Second, the books page should always reflect the real program shape even when the available list is short; better scarcity than false abundance. Third, the store should sell only what the press can stand behind materially and aesthetically. Books when they exist. Prints when they mean something. Fourth, the site should keep first-party list capture and external feed routing in the same voice so that updates culture does not split into one solemn institutional channel and one generic newsletter channel. Fifth, the public language should never collapse into "small press" boilerplate. St. Expedite is not asking to be recognized as one more cultural nonprofit with better typography.

## VIII. Editorial Process and Standards

Editorially, the press should remain slow in judgment and fast in decision. Those are not opposites. Slow in judgment means the reading must attend to the actual work rather than to the pressures surrounding it. Fast in decision means that once the reading is clear, the press should not create committees or waiting rituals merely to mimic legitimacy. The work comes in, the work is read, the terms are named, and the answer is given.

Selection should be based on sentence pressure, formal control, symbolic intelligence, tonal coherence, and rereadability. For fiction, this means scene architecture, weather, verbal music, and seriousness of consequence. For poetry, it means cadence before posture. For criticism, it means diagnosis rather than recitation. For recovery work, it means present necessity rather than heritage sentiment. For translation, it means force-preservation rather than bourgeois smoothing.

The press should prefer a small number of books that can survive direct contact over a larger number of books that depend on ambient goodwill. It should also resist the false generosity of vague interest. One of the institutional diseases of contemporary publishing is that nobody will say no clearly. St. Expedite should be unusually clear in both acceptance and refusal.

## IX. Research Program and Archival Method

The recovery and translation lines require a research discipline equal to their claims. That means bibliographic work, rights assessment, public-domain timing, source-comparison, and textual-condition notes. It also means understanding that absence from circulation is never merely an accident. Some authors were neglected because taste changed. Others were neglected because their work could not be neatly assimilated to later institutional narratives. Others survive only in degraded forms because no one with actual authority cared enough to preserve them properly.

This is where the research-page method properly enters the press. Variant search terms, period vocabulary, archive bias, textual damage, and conspicuous absences all matter. A St. Expedite recovery edition should therefore include a defensible account of what text was used, what was emended or silently preserved, what other editions were rejected, and why the book warrants return now. The afterword or editorial note should sharpen the argument, not fill pages with museum-talk.

The current named acquisition cluster should be expanded gradually by method, not by indiscriminate enthusiasm. Early Southern modernists, neglected agrarian-adjacent figures who exceed caricature, poets misfiled by ideological simplification, and writers whose formal intelligence has outlived the schools that ignored them all belong in scope. But the press should not become a salvage yard. Republication is selection, not rescue theater.

## X. Timeline and Horizon

The developmental sequence visible on the live site remains the correct one. Phase one is presence and proof. That phase is active now. The site exists, the basic public lanes exist, the first books and objects can exist, the donation mechanism exists, and the lab exists. The immediate work inside phase one is to increase density: stronger catalog gravity, more exact public prose, clearer release sequencing, cleaner reader routing, and a growing sense that this is a house rather than a homepage.

Phase two is RICE Magazine. It begins only when production, handling, and enough recurring attention are in place to prevent the magazine from becoming a romantic burden. When it starts, it should start as a real object with cadence, not as an apology. The goal is to create public rhythm, attract serious contributors, and make the press legible as a discourse engine rather than a static catalog.

Phase three is the fellowship mechanism. This should not imitate the prestige-residency model or the university's patronage rituals. The point is not to produce lifestyle branding for "emerging writers." The point is to buy time for serious people outside the grant circuit and outside the academy's soft disciplinary enclosures. A supported-writer mechanism can begin small. It does not need an estate, a donor gala, or a national call. It needs criteria, revenue, and the courage to choose.

Phase four is networked ecology. That is the point at which the press can begin entering co-publication arrangements, venue relationships, guild-like associations, shared marketplace logic, or distributed catalog experiments without becoming diffuse. Commonsplace belongs here. So do fellow-traveler presses, local and transregional reading rooms, seasonal programming, and a serious attempt to give non-university literary life durable infrastructure.

The planning horizon from 2026 through 2030 should therefore remain cumulative. In 2026 the task is consolidation, copy discipline, release clarity, and doctrinal coherence between site, vault, and catalog. In 2027 the threshold is whether RICE can launch without self-harm and whether the direct-sales culture is strong enough to sustain recurrence. In 2028 the press should deepen the recovery and translation pipelines, test whether small fellowship mechanisms can operate cleanly, and formalize the relation between catalog, lab, and Commonsplace. By 2029 and 2030 the real question is whether St. Expedite can move from strong founder-led imprint to the first durable cell of a larger ecology without surrendering its speed, standards, or nerve.

Beyond 2030 the true ambition is not bigness. It is repeatability. The best outcome is a machine small enough to stay intelligent and large enough to keep reproducing serious literary life: books, journal, objects, correspondence, supported time, archival recovery, public argument, and eventually a federation of adjacent efforts that recognize one another without being flattened into a brand family.

## XI. Relation to `Thinking/`

The press is fed by a deeper theory layer that should remain adjacent but not confused with front-of-house publishing. `Thinking/Ontology/Primacy of the Agent.md`, `Thinking/Ontology/Not Another Media Property.md`, `Thinking/Ontology/Romanticism as Ontological Gate.md`, `Thinking/Ontology/Commonsplace Positionalty.md`, and the `Thinking/Anglosic Grammar/Solemn Providence/` materials all function as design pressure. They help determine what the press selects, how the lab behaves, how services are framed, and what Commonsplace may eventually become.

The move that relocated old `Editorial/` and `Land Series/` materials into `Thinking/` was therefore correct. Those files are conceptual substrate, not live press administration. The press should metabolize them selectively into books, essays, instruments, magazine dossiers, translation rationale, or institutional design. It should not drag the entire theory stack onto the shop floor.

## XII. Immediate Directives

In immediate practical terms, the press should keep the St. Expedite folder flat and canonical, keep this document and the Commonsplace whitepaper as the two controlling synthesis files, preserve the clean separation between publishing surfaces and theory surfaces, and keep the site aligned with what the institution can actually support now. The live catalog should remain short, the language exact, the store intentional, and the services line selective. RICE should remain planned until the economics justify it, but the doctrine and naming should be considered settled. Recovery research should continue in a disciplined way, with text-establishment and rights logic documented as candidates sharpen into actual editions.

## XIII. Closing Position

St. Expedite Press is best understood as a founding node rather than a finished institution. It does not need immediate scale. It needs density, recurrence, courage, and memory. It needs to print difficult work, recover neglected work, refuse flattening, and buy time wherever possible for the kind of literary seriousness that contemporary institutions increasingly know only how to simulate.

If it succeeds, the result will not be a lifestyle brand, not a respectable regional nonprofit, and not another boutique micropress that disappears after three seasons of enthusiasm. The result will be a small but durable literary machine in New Orleans, operating in the key of Night, capable of publishing, selecting, arguing, recovering, sponsoring, and eventually federating without surrendering the standards that made it worth building in the first place.
